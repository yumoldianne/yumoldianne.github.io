How close is close enough? Property type and cluster nuances in the price effects of LRT-1 proximity

Modeling 72,000+ Metro Manila property listings with PCA, clustering, and geographically weighted regression to figure out whether — and where — being near a rail line actually raises land value.

Published 2026 · Elsevier
·
10 min read
·
Spatial Regression GWR Clustering Urban Economics
72,019 listings · 5 property types · 1 rail line

Metro Manila needs an estimated USD 13 billion in rail infrastructure, and the government can't fund all of it through appropriations and foreign aid alone. One proposed lever is land value capture (LVC): since better transit access tends to raise nearby property values, a share of that uplift can, in theory, be captured to help pay for the transit itself. The catch is that "in theory" is doing a lot of work — you first have to prove the uplift exists, measure it accurately, and show it's not just noise from a property's other characteristics.

TL;DR: I co-built a segmented modeling pipeline — PCA → clustering → geographically weighted regression — on 72,019 Lamudi property listings along Metro Manila's LRT-1 line. LRT-1 proximity does raise prices, but its effect is smaller than a property's own characteristics, highly inconsistent across property types, and concentrated in specific clusters and locations — evidence against a one-size-fits-all LVC policy.

The question: does "close to the station" even mean one thing?

The naive version of this analysis is a single hedonic regression: property price on distance to the nearest LRT-1 station, plus controls. The problem is that "controls" is hiding an enormous amount of heterogeneity — a landed house in Cavite and a Makati CBD condo respond to transit access completely differently, and even within condos, a unit in the central business district behaves unlike one in a residential fringe. A single global coefficient on distance-to-station would average all of that away and answer a question nobody actually asked.

So instead of one model, we built a segmented pipeline: reduce dimensionality per property category, cluster properties into locally coherent groups, then fit spatially-varying regressions within each cluster. That structure is what lets the paper's title — "how close is close enough" — actually get answered differently for a house near the northern terminal than for a commercial unit in Makati.

Data: 72,019 listings, five property types, four data sources

Property records came from Lamudi.com.ph (August 2024 snapshot), covering houses, condominiums, apartments, commercial real estate, and land along LRT-1's existing and planned Cavite Extension stations. We layered in three more data sources through QGIS and public APIs:

  • OpenStreetMap — distance to nearest LRT-1 station, plus counts of schools, malls, hospitals, ATMs, and bus terminals within a 2 km radius of each property.
  • DTI's Cities and Municipalities Competitiveness Index (CMCI) — city-level socio-economic indicators, narrowed down (after VIF screening for multicollinearity) to six Economic Dynamism features: local economy growth and size, business organization presence, financial deepening, employment generation, and safety-compliant businesses.
  • Property listing text — bedrooms, bathrooms, floor area, land area, and price (the outcome variable).

After deduplication and outlier removal, 59,210 of the original 72,019 listings remained across the five categories, with condominiums (27,266) and houses (33,560) dominating and apartments (304) badly underrepresented — a limitation that shows up later in the apartment results.

Methods: PCA → clustering → segmented GWR

For each property category, we ran Principal Component Analysis on the non-price, non-distance features to collapse redundant amenity and socio-economic variables into a handful of components — deliberately excluding price (to avoid data leakage) and LRT distance (to preserve it as a named, interpretable predictor rather than dissolving it into an abstract component). Four principal components consistently captured most of the variance; for condos, PC1 (60% of variance) tracked macroeconomic indicators, PC2 (18%) tracked structural size, PC3 (15%) tracked transit/school access, and PC4 (8%) tracked healthcare access. 

# Segmented pipeline, per property category
pca_features = PCA(n_components=4).fit_transform(X_minus_price_and_distance)
clusters = KMeans(k=elbow_optimal).fit_predict(pca_features)  # also tried BIRCH

for cluster_id in clusters.unique():
    gwr = GWR(y=price[cluster_id], X=[distance_to_lrt, *controls])
    local_coeffs = gwr.fit()  # coefficient surface, not a single number

A Hopkins test confirmed strong clustering tendency across all five property types before we committed to K-Means and BIRCH. The optimal number of clusters per category was set via the elbow method — three for houses and land, five for apartments and commercial real estate, three for condos. Finally, Geographically Weighted Regression (GWR) was fit inside each cluster, producing a local coefficient for LRT distance at every location rather than one number for the whole city — which is the only way to answer "how close is close enough" as a spatial question instead of a single average.

Results: transit access matters, but unevenly

For condominiums, the clusters split almost exactly along a CBD-vs-periphery line: Cluster 0 (Makati CBD) shows LRT distance coefficients averaging −39.65 (closer to a station → higher price), while Clusters 1 and 2 — further from the CBD — show small, inconsistent, even slightly positive average effects. Model fit was strong across all three (R² of 0.79–0.91), but the story is squarely "transit access matters where employment access already matters, and washes out elsewhere."

Across the other property types, the pattern was just as type-specific:

  • Houses — proximity effects are strongly positive near the line's northern terminal, but mixed elsewhere; other factors dominate outside that zone.
  • Apartments — generally show prices falling as distance to LRT-1 shrinks, though the effect is weak and the category's tiny sample (304 listings, with one cluster too sparse for GWR to even run) limits how much weight this result can carry.
  • Commercial real estate — CBD clusters show near-zero sensitivity to transit (tenants there prioritize road access and parking over pedestrian transit), while peripheral clusters in Pasig/Taguig/Pateros show large, volatile, mixed-sign coefficients driven by a more heterogeneous mix of small retail and food-service tenants.
  • Land — the clearest positive, consistent relationship: value rises closer to stations, concentrated near the northern terminal and the newly built southern (Cavite) extension — areas where anticipated development, not existing amenities, seems to be pricing in future accessibility.
Headline finding: LRT-1 accessibility does raise property prices on average, but its effect is secondary to a property's own characteristics and highly heterogeneous by both location and property type — condos only show a real transit premium in Makati; apartments barely respond at all; land and low-density housing near the terminals respond the most.

What this means for financing rail with land value capture

The paper's methodology answers a research question, but its findings are aimed squarely at a policy decision: what should a land value capture instrument in Metro Manila actually look like? The evidence argues against a uniform, corridor-wide levy and toward something spatially and categorically targeted:

  • Land parcels and low-density houses near the northern terminal and the Cavite Extension show the strongest, most consistent uplift — these are the best candidates for betterment levies or land value increment financing, the most theoretically defensible LVC instruments because they tax the value increase directly rather than taxing improvements.
  • Makati condominiums show a real but geographically narrow premium — suited to development charges or density-bonus schemes tied specifically to high-rise projects in that corridor, not a blanket condo tax.
  • Apartments and most commercial clusters show weak or inconsistent transit effects — applying a uniform station-buffer levy here would tax categories with little transit-linked uplift while potentially under-taxing the categories (land, terminal-adjacent housing) where the uplift is real and large.

In other words, the clustering step isn't just a modeling convenience — it's the thing that makes the policy recommendation possible at all. A single city-wide hedonic coefficient would have told policymakers "proximity raises prices, tax accordingly," which sounds actionable but would have produced an LVC instrument that over-taxes some property owners and leaves real value on the table for others.

Limitations and what I'd do differently

Lamudi listings are asking prices, not transaction prices, which skews toward mid-to-high-end inventory and introduces a directional bias toward overstated absolute price levels — though the relative spatial patterns and cluster comparisons should hold even if absolute levels are inflated, provided that markup is roughly constant across the study area. The data is also cross-sectional (a single August 2024 snapshot), so we can't observe how the market responded to the actual opening of new stations over time — only compare locations that already differ in current accessibility. Socio-economic indicators from DTI are reported at the city level, which is coarser than the vicinity-level analysis we ran everything else at, and several LGUs (Malabon, Navotas, Muntinlupa, and others) had to be dropped for incomplete data. Future work with actual transaction records and panel data around the Cavite Extension's opening would let this move from a cross-sectional estimate to a real before/after causal comparison.
