Private Wealth x DS: unlocking customer insights for hyper-personalization

A five-level framework for turning raw credit card transaction logs into client-level signals a relationship manager can actually act on — from a single column of numbers to an AI-generated "who's about to get rich" score.

2,419 clients · 5 variable tiers · 1 personalization engine

A private wealth team's client profiles usually come from a form filled out once, years ago — declared net worth bracket, a few checkboxes about interests, maybe a relationship manager's notes. Meanwhile every one of those clients is generating a much richer, constantly-updating signal: their actual credit card transactions. This project was about closing that gap — turning transaction-level data into variables the business could use for segmentation, retention, and personalization, without ever needing a client to fill out another form.

TL;DR: We built a five-tier variable pipeline — from raw transaction fields to AI/ML-generated scores — on top of 2,419 private wealth clients' credit card data. The headline finding: 73% of clients had no usable net-worth classification in the existing CRM fields, meaning the bank's most important segmentation dimension was mostly missing — and transaction-derived variables could fill that gap.

The task

The brief had three parts: validate or redefine current client profiles using real transaction patterns instead of stale declared data; flag potential high-value clients and clients at risk of disengagement; and give relationship managers enough signal to personalize outreach and marketing around actual client lifestyles rather than generic wealth-tier scripts.

The approach: five levels of increasing sophistication

Rather than jumping straight to a model, we built variables in layers — each layer reusing the one below it — so that every insight stayed traceable back to a raw, auditable transaction field. That matters a lot in a regulated wealth-management context: a relationship manager (and a compliance team) needs to be able to ask "why does the system think this client is a flight risk?" and get an answer that bottoms out in real transactions, not a black box.

L1

Simple, non-derived

Raw fields straight from the transaction record: amount, count, merchant category.

L2

Basic derived

Simple aggregations over time — average spend, total transaction count, spending diversity across merchant categories.

L3

Complex derived

Behavioral ratios — travel-spend proportion, philanthropic engagement score, wealth-transfer score.

L4

External enrichment

Government and third-party data joined in — DTI economic indicators by city, sustainability rankings by brand.

L5

AI/ML-generated

Learned scores — anomaly detection, merchant-similarity embeddings, a "future high spender" predictive score.

Levels 1–2: what the raw data already told us

Even before any derived logic, the raw fields exposed a data-quality problem worth flagging on its own: of the 2,419 clients analyzed, only 27% had a usable AUM-based net worth classification (Mass Affluent, High/Very High/Ultra High Net Worth) — the rest were UNCLASSIFIED. That's the CRM's core segmentation field, largely empty, for a three-quarters of the book.

From there, two simple derived flags — High Spender (top 10% by total transaction amount) and Frequent Transactor (top 10% by transaction count) — turned out to be almost the same population (2,048 of ~2,290 flagged clients were both), which told us spend volume and spend frequency aren't independent signals for this client base — a useful simplification for whatever segmentation logic comes next. A Diversity of Spending score (merchant category count per client, mean ≈ 20 categories) gave a first pass at lifestyle breadth: clients spread thin across categories versus concentrated in a few.

Level 3: behavior ratios that map to specific product lines

This is where the variables stopped being generic statistics and started mapping directly onto products the bank could actually sell. A travel-spend proportion (mean ≈ 10% of transaction value going to travel merchants) flags clients for travel-benefit personalization. A philanthropic engagement score — percentage of spend going to charities, religious institutions, and similar merchants — flags clients for donor-advised funds and impact investing conversations, though the data here was sparse (95.7% of clients showed no measurable philanthropic spend on-card, which is itself informative: most philanthropic giving likely happens off-card and wouldn't be visible to a transaction-based model). A wealth-transfer score (proportion of transactions tied to wealth-transfer activity) flags clients worth an estate-planning or trust-management conversation — 14% of clients showed at least some wealth-transfer signal, cutting across all declared net-worth tiers, including the 73% previously unclassified.

Level 4: bringing in the outside world

Two external data sources extended the picture beyond what a client's own transactions could show. The DTI's Cities and Municipalities Competitiveness Index layered in regional economic context — employment rates, financial deepening, business stability — letting the team flag which cities and municipalities have thriving business environments likely correlated with rising disposable income, useful for prioritizing branch or advisory expansion. A brand-sustainability ranking (Good On You) let us flag eco-conscious spending behavior — clients whose purchases skew toward ethically- and environmentally-ranked brands — as a segment for sustainable investment products and green-brand partnerships.

Level 5: where the ML actually lives

The final tier used the lower levels as inputs to three learned scores:

  • Anomaly score — flags transaction patterns that deviate from a client's own historical baseline. The dual read is deliberate: an anomaly can mean fraud risk, or it can mean a client's spending has jumped in a way that doesn't match their declared wealth tier yet — an early signal of a client entering high-net-worth territory before the CRM catches up.
  • Merchant similarity score — an embedding-based measure of which merchants share overlapping customer bases (e.g. Cebu Pacific ↔ Tim Ho Wan ↔ Tudor Podium), used to identify partnership and cross-sell opportunities with brands whose customers already look like the bank's private wealth clients.
  • Future high spending score — a predictive score combining per-transaction spend and recent spend growth to flag clients on the verge of a meaningful wealth increase, before they cross into the next declared tier.

What this means for the business

The project's real deliverable wasn't a single model — it was a reusable pipeline that turns a commodity data asset (card transactions) into a segmentation and personalization layer the bank didn't have before. Three concrete business implications came out of it:

  • The CRM's core segmentation field is unreliable. With 73% of clients unclassified by declared net worth, any strategy built on that field alone is working with roughly a quarter of the picture. Transaction-derived variables are a way to backfill that gap without waiting on clients to update forms.
  • Retention and growth signals can run ahead of the CRM. The anomaly and future-high-spending scores are explicitly designed to catch clients before their behavior shows up in a wealth-tier reclassification — turning private wealth outreach from reactive (respond once someone is already flagged high-net-worth) to proactive.
  • Personalization has a product on the other end of every variable. Every Level 3+ score was scoped to map onto something sellable — travel benefits, donor-advised funds, estate planning, sustainable investing, merchant partnerships — rather than being an abstract cluster label with no obvious next action for a relationship manager.
Design principle that mattered most: every AI/ML-generated variable at Level 5 had to be traceable back through Levels 1–4 to a real transaction. In a wealth-management context, an unexplainable score is a liability, not an insight.
